Vectors

Direction and magnitude, dot product and angle

A vector is just an arrow. It has a direction and a length, and that's the whole definition. Put two in the same plane, and a number called the dot product tells you how much they agree: multiply matching components and add them up. If both point roughly the same way, you get a positive number. Perpendicular vectors give exactly zero. Pointing opposite ways gives a negative.

This matters because the same quantity equals \(|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta\), which is why the dot product appears in nearly every formula involving angles. Drag the sliders to adjust u and v and watch the angle update live.

Key Formulas
\(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=u_xv_x+u_yv_y\)

\(|\mathbf{u}|=\sqrt{u_x^2+u_y^2}\)

\(\theta=\arccos\!\dfrac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|}\)

Linear Algebra · Chapter 2

Matrix × Vector

How a 2×2 matrix moves a vector through the plane

A 2×2 matrix transforms a vector by mixing its components. The first column tells you where the x-axis unit vector lands after the transformation. The second column tells you where the y-axis unit vector lands. Everything else follows by linearity: scale the columns and add them.

The light arrow is your input vector \(\mathbf{v}\). The dark arrow is \(M\mathbf{v}\). Four numbers produce stretching, shearing, reflection, and rotation. Set a=0, b=-1, c=1, d=0 and you get a 90° counterclockwise rotation.

Matrix Action
\(\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}ax{+}by\\cx{+}dy\end{pmatrix}\)

Linear Algebra · Chapter 3

Determinants

The signed area of the column parallelogram

The determinant of a 2×2 matrix has a concrete geometric meaning: it's the signed area of the parallelogram formed by the two column vectors. Positive means the columns go counterclockwise from each other. Negative means clockwise. Zero means they're collinear and the parallelogram has collapsed to a line.

Adjust the four entries and watch the parallelogram change. When the columns are nearly parallel, the area shrinks toward zero. This is also when the matrix becomes singular: no inverse exists, and linear systems it produces either have no solution or infinitely many.

Formula
\(\det\!\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}=ad-bc\)

columns: (a,c) and (b,d)

Linear Algebra · Chapter 4

Linear Systems

Two equations, two lines, one intersection

A 2×2 linear system is two lines. The solution is the point where they cross. Three outcomes are possible: one intersection (unique solution), parallel lines that never meet (no solution), or the same line where every point satisfies both equations (infinitely many solutions).

Adjust the coefficients using the sliders. Each equation is \(a_ix + b_iy = c_i\). The determinant of the coefficient matrix tells you immediately which case applies: nonzero means a unique crossing exists, zero means something has gone wrong geometrically.

Cramer's Rule
\(x=\dfrac{c_1b_2-c_2b_1}{a_1b_2-a_2b_1}\)

\(y=\dfrac{a_1c_2-a_2c_1}{a_1b_2-a_2b_1}\)

Linear Algebra · Chapter 5

Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

The directions a transformation leaves unchanged

When a matrix transforms a vector, most vectors get rotated in the process. Eigenvectors don't. They only stretch or flip, staying on the same line through the origin. The stretch factor is the eigenvalue. A negative eigenvalue means the vector reverses direction rather than just scaling.

The faint circle is the unit circle. The light ellipse shows where the unit circle maps under the transformation. The dark lines are the eigenvector directions. Try a=0, b=-1, c=1, d=0 for a pure rotation where no real eigenvectors exist at all.

Characteristic Equation
\(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\)

\(\lambda^2-(a{+}d)\lambda\)
\(+(ad-bc)=0\)

Δ<0 means complex λ

Linear Transformations

How a matrix deforms the entire coordinate plane

A 2×2 matrix deforms the whole plane at once. Three things always remain true: straight lines stay straight, evenly spaced parallel lines stay evenly spaced, and the origin stays fixed. Those three constraints define a linear transformation.

The faint grid shows the original coordinate system. The colored grid shows where every point ends up after the transformation. The shaded unit square shows how area has scaled; its area is always \(|\det(A)|\). When the determinant is negative, the transformation flips orientation like a reflection.

Column Interpretation
\(A\mathbf{e}_1=\text{col}_1(A)\)
\(A\mathbf{e}_2=\text{col}_2(A)\)

✎ About This Module

Math Explorer is a collection of interactive modules for IB, AP, and college-level math. The linear algebra section covers vectors, determinants, eigenvalues, and transformations through canvas-based visualizations built without any external graphics libraries.

Most textbook treatments work through pages of notation before showing a single picture. These modules try to put the geometry first, so the intuition arrives before the algebra. The math is still there. It just shows up after you already know what it's describing.
