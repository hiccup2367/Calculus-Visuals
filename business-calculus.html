Supply & Demand

Where quantity supplied meets quantity demanded

The demand curve \(P_D(q)=a-bq\) captures a simple truth: as price goes up, buyers buy less. The supply curve \(P_S(q)=c+dq\) captures the opposite: sellers need higher prices to justify producing more. Where the two lines cross is the market equilibrium \((q^*,p^*)\).

You can solve for \(q^*\) directly: set \(a-bq=c+dq\) and rearrange to \(q^*=\tfrac{a-c}{b+d}\). The consumer surplus (green area) is what buyers gain because they paid less than they were willing to. Producer surplus (lighter area) is the analogous gain for sellers. Together they sum to total surplus, the economy's net benefit from the market.

Try shifting the demand intercept \(a\) upward to simulate a demand shock. Price and quantity both rise. Now increase the supply slope \(d\) instead. Price rises but quantity falls. Two very different causes, two very different effects.

Equilibrium
\(q^*=\dfrac{a-c}{b+d}\)

\(p^*=a-b\cdot q^*\)

CS \(=\tfrac{1}{2}q^*(a-p^*)\)
PS \(=\tfrac{1}{2}q^*(p^*-c)\)

Marginal Analysis

The derivative of cost, revenue, and profit

Profit is \(\pi(q)=TR(q)-TC(q)\). For a competitive firm, \(TR=Pq\) is a straight line with slope \(P\). The total cost curve bends: fixed costs create a vertical intercept, and eventually diminishing returns push costs up faster than output grows.

To maximize profit, set \(\pi'(q)=0\). That's the same as \(MR=MC\): produce an extra unit only if the revenue it generates exceeds what it costs to make. The visualization shades green where TR exceeds TC (profit) and orange where it falls below (loss). The optimal quantity is where the gap is widest.

You can also enter a custom cost function. Try something like 50 + 3*q + 0.2*q^2 and see how the shape of the TC curve changes what the optimal quantity looks like.

Price Elasticity of Demand

How sensitively quantity responds to a price change

Price elasticity \(E=\tfrac{dQ}{dP}\cdot\tfrac{P}{Q}\) answers the question: if I raise my price by 1%, how much do my sales drop? When \(|E|>1\), a price increase actually lowers total revenue because the volume loss outweighs the margin gain. When \(|E|<1\), it raises revenue. The sweet spot, where revenue is maximized, is \(|E|=1\).

For linear demand \(P=a-bq\), elasticity equals \(P/(a-P)\). This means the top half of the demand curve is elastic and the bottom half inelastic, with the midpoint price \(P=a/2\) being unit elastic every time regardless of the slope. The curve below is colored by region: teal = elastic, amber = unit elastic, green = inelastic.

Break-Even Analysis

The quantity where revenue exactly covers cost

Every firm carries fixed costs \(FC\) that exist regardless of output: rent, equipment, staff. Beyond those, each unit costs \(vc\) to produce. So \(TC(q)=FC+vc\cdot q\). Revenue is \(TR(q)=P\cdot q\). Set them equal and you get the break-even quantity: \(q_{BE}=FC/(P-vc)\).

The denominator \(P-vc\) is the contribution margin. Each unit sold contributes that amount toward covering fixed costs, and then toward profit once FC is recovered. If the contribution margin goes negative, you lose money on every unit and no volume of sales can save you.

Drag the current quantity slider to simulate your production level. Green is profit, orange is loss. The margin of safety tells you how many units of buffer you have above break-even.

\(q_{BE}=\dfrac{FC}{P-vc}\)

Profit Optimization

Maximizing profit where marginal revenue meets marginal cost

For a competitive firm, the market price is fixed and MR equals P: a flat horizontal line. The firm's only job is to find where MC crosses that line from below. The profit rectangle has height equal to (P minus ATC) and width equal to q*; its area is economic profit when positive and a loss when negative.

A monopolist faces the full demand curve, so TR is a downward-opening parabola and MR slopes at twice the rate of demand. The monopolist picks the quantity where MR = MC, then reads the price off the demand curve above. The result is higher price and lower quantity than a competitive market would produce, with the gap being deadweight loss.

You can enter a custom cost function to test your own TC curve. The module finds the optimal quantity numerically by scanning for the minimum of |MR - MC|.

Consumer & Producer Surplus

Welfare areas and deadweight loss from price controls

At equilibrium, both sides of every transaction are better off: buyers pay less than they were willing to pay, and sellers receive more than their minimum acceptable price. Those gaps are consumer surplus and producer surplus. They are integrals: \(CS=\int_0^{q^*}[P_D(q)-p^*]\,dq\) and similarly for PS.

When a government sets a price floor (above equilibrium) or a price ceiling (below it), the quantity traded changes. Total surplus shrinks. The lost value is the deadweight loss: transactions that would have benefited both parties but now do not happen. No policy transfers that value elsewhere. It simply disappears.

Drag the regulated price away from equilibrium and watch the red triangle grow.

Surplus Areas
\(CS=\int_0^{q_T}[P_D-p]\,dq\)

\(PS=\int_0^{q_T}[p-P_S]\,dq\)

\(DWL=TS^*-CS-PS\)

Present Value of an Income Stream

Discounting future cash flows back to today

A dollar received in the future is worth less than a dollar today. Not because of some abstract finance rule, but because a dollar today can be invested at rate \(r\) to grow into \(e^{rt}\) dollars by time \(t\). Working backwards: a dollar at time \(t\) is worth \(e^{-rt}\) today.

The present value of a continuous income stream \(R(t)\) over horizon \(T\) is \(PV=\int_0^T R(t)e^{-rt}\,dt\). The solid curve is \(R(t)\) (actual cash flow). The dashed curve is \(R(t)e^{-rt}\) (what those flows are worth today). The shaded area under the dashed curve is the PV. A high discount rate compresses that dashed curve fast: income 15 years out barely registers.

Present Value
\(PV=\displaystyle\int_0^T R(t)e^{-rt}\,dt\)

Constant \(R\):
\(PV=\dfrac{R}{r}(1-e^{-rT})\)

Compound Growth

As compounding frequency grows, discrete growth converges to \(A=Pe^{rt}\)

If you invest \(P\) dollars at annual rate \(r\) compounded \(n\) times per year, after \(t\) years you have \(A_n(t)=P(1+r/n)^{nt}\). As \(n\to\infty\), this converges to \(A(t)=Pe^{rt}\). The limit exists because \(\lim_{n\to\infty}(1+r/n)^n=e^r\), which is one of the more satisfying results in calculus.

In practice, the difference between monthly and continuous compounding is small. The gap between annual and continuous is more interesting. At high rates and long horizons it can be dramatic. All five curves are drawn here simultaneously. The key insight is not that continuous compounding is common but that it gives you the mathematical ceiling on what compounding can achieve.

Compounding
Discrete: \(A=P\!\left(1+\dfrac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}\)

Continuous: \(A=Pe^{rt}\)

EAR: \(e^r-1\)

Economic Order Quantity

Minimizing total inventory cost by balancing order frequency against holding cost

Every inventory decision involves a tradeoff. Order more at once and you save on ordering costs (fewer orders per year) but pay more in holding costs (more stock sitting in a warehouse). Order less frequently and the reverse happens. The Economic Order Quantity is the order size that minimizes total annual cost.

With annual demand \(D\), ordering cost \(S\) per order, and holding cost \(H\) per unit per year, total annual cost is \(TC(q)=\tfrac{D}{q}\cdot S+\tfrac{q}{2}\cdot H\). Setting \(TC'(q)=0\) gives the exact minimum: \(q^*=\sqrt{\tfrac{2DS}{H}}\). This is one of the cleanest applications of single-variable optimization in business math.

The two component curves form a classic U-shape when added together. The minimum sits right where ordering cost equals holding cost, which is worth noting: at the optimum, you split your costs evenly between the two sources.

EOQ Formula
\(TC(q)=\dfrac{DS}{q}+\dfrac{qH}{2}\)

\(q^*=\sqrt{\dfrac{2DS}{H}}\)

At optimum:
\(\dfrac{DS}{q^*}=\dfrac{q^*H}{2}\)

Lorenz Curve & Gini Coefficient

Measuring income inequality with an integral

The Lorenz curve \(L(x)\) plots cumulative income share against cumulative population share. If income were perfectly equal, \(L(x)=x\): the bottom 40% of the population would earn exactly 40% of total income. In practice, \(L(x)\) lies below the diagonal, bowed toward the bottom-right corner. The more bowed it is, the more unequal the distribution.

The Gini coefficient quantifies this: \(G=2\int_0^1[x-L(x)]\,dx\). It is twice the area between the equality line and the Lorenz curve. \(G=0\) means perfect equality; \(G=1\) means one person has everything. Real countries typically fall between 0.25 and 0.60. This is the cleanest use of a definite integral in applied social science.

The curve presets match stylized distributions. The power-law form \(L(x)=x^k\) is the most tractable: higher \(k\) means more inequality. The Gini for \(L(x)=x^k\) is exactly \((k-1)/(k+1)\).

\(G=2\int_0^1[x-L(x)]\,dx\)

\(G=\dfrac{k-1}{k+1}\)

\(G=0\): perfect equality
\(G=1\): one person all

Cobb-Douglas Production

Isoquants, returns to scale, and the MRTS

The Cobb-Douglas production function \(Q=AL^{\alpha}K^{\beta}\) is the workhorse of macroeconomics and production theory. \(L\) is labor, \(K\) is capital. The exponents \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) measure each input's elasticity of output: a 1% increase in labor raises output by \(\alpha\)%.

When \(\alpha+\beta=1\), the function exhibits constant returns to scale: doubling both inputs exactly doubles output. When \(\alpha+\beta>1\), there are increasing returns (economies of scale). When the sum falls below 1, there are decreasing returns.

An isoquant is a curve connecting all input combinations that produce the same output level. Think of it as an indifference curve for a firm. The slope at any point along the isoquant is the MRTS (marginal rate of technical substitution): how much capital you can trade away per unit of labor while holding output constant. It equals \((\alpha K)/(\beta L)\).

Cobb-Douglas
\(Q=AL^\alpha K^\beta\)

  =1: constant
  >1: increasing
  <1: decreasing

Price Discrimination

Extracting consumer surplus by charging different prices to different buyers

Under standard monopoly pricing, the firm leaves money on the table: buyers who would pay more than the posted price get a surplus the firm cannot capture. Price discrimination is the strategy of charging different prices to different buyers to recover some of that surplus.

First-degree (perfect) price discrimination charges each buyer exactly their willingness to pay. The firm captures all consumer surplus and produces the competitive quantity. No deadweight loss. In practice this requires knowing every buyer's reservation price, which is almost never possible.

Third-degree price discrimination splits buyers into two or more identifiable groups (students vs. adults, domestic vs. foreign) and sets a separate price for each, choosing the price that maximizes profit within each segment: \(MR_1=MR_2=MC\). The group with more elastic demand pays less.

Business Calculus applies the tools of differential and integral calculus to economic decisions. Firms optimize profit, markets reach equilibrium, investors discount future cash flows, and policies create or destroy welfare. These 12 modules cover the core Business Calculus curriculum with live visualizations.

The thread running through all of them is the same idea: think at the margin. Every business decision, from pricing to inventory to hiring, is best analyzed by asking what happens to costs, revenues, and profits when you change the decision variable by one small unit. The derivative makes that precise.
