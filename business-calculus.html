Supply & Demand

Where quantity supplied meets quantity demanded — the market equilibrium

In a competitive market, the demand curve \(P_D(q)=a-bq\) shows the highest price consumers will pay for quantity \(q\) (downward sloping — higher price, less demanded). The supply curve \(P_S(q)=c+dq\) shows the minimum price sellers accept (upward sloping — higher price, more supplied).

The market equilibrium \((q^*,p^*)\) is where these curves intersect: \(a-bq^*=c+dq^*\Rightarrow q^*=\tfrac{a-c}{b+d}\). Consumer surplus (green) is the area between the demand curve and \(p^*\) — the benefit buyers receive beyond what they pay. Producer surplus (lighter) is the area between \(p^*\) and the supply curve — seller profit above their minimum acceptable price.

Drag any of the four parameters to shift the curves. Notice how supply shocks and demand shifts each change equilibrium price, quantity, and the distribution of surplus.

Equilibrium
\(P_D(q)=a-bq\)
\(P_S(q)=c+dq\)

\(q^*=\dfrac{a-c}{b+d}\)

\(p^*=a-b\cdot q^*\)

CS = ½q*(a−p*)
PS = ½q*(p*−c)

Marginal Analysis

The derivative of cost, revenue, and profit — optimizing at the margin

In competitive markets a firm is a price taker: total revenue is simply \(TR(q)=P\cdot q\) (a straight line) and marginal revenue \(MR=P\) is constant. The firm chooses output \(q\) to maximize profit \(\pi(q)=TR(q)-TC(q)\), where \(TC(q)\) includes both fixed and variable costs.

The optimum satisfies \(\pi'(q)=0\), equivalently \(MR=MC\): produce another unit only if the revenue it adds exceeds its marginal cost. The shaded gap at any \(q\) between TR and TC visually shows the profit level. Drag the Market Price slider to see how profitability changes as the firm's environment shifts.

The green region is economic profit, the orange region is a loss. At the optimum, the TR line and TC curve have equal slopes.

Profit Condition
\(\pi(q)=TR-TC\)

Maximize when:
\(\pi'(q)=0\)
\(\Leftrightarrow MR=MC\)

\(MR=P\) (competitive)
\(MC=TC'(q)\)

Price Elasticity of Demand

How sensitively quantity responds to a price change

Price elasticity \(E=\tfrac{dQ}{dP}\cdot\tfrac{P}{Q}\) measures the percentage change in quantity demanded per 1% change in price. Because demand slopes downward \(dQ/dP<0\), so \(E\le 0\); we often report \(|E|\). When \(|E|>1\) demand is elastic — consumers are sensitive and a price rise hurts total revenue. When \(|E|<1\) demand is inelastic — total revenue rises with price. At \(|E|=1\) (unit elastic) total revenue is maximized.

For linear inverse demand \(P=a-bq\): the quantity demanded at price \(P\) is \(q=(a-P)/b\), so \(|E|=P/(a-P)\). At the midpoint price \(P=a/2\) the elasticity is exactly 1. Above that point demand is elastic; below it, inelastic.

The demand curve is colored by elasticity: teal = elastic, amber = unit elastic, green = inelastic. Move the price slider and read how elasticity changes.

Point Elasticity
\(E=\dfrac{dQ}{dP}\cdot\dfrac{P}{Q}\)

Linear demand:
\(|E|=\dfrac{P}{a-P}\)

\(|E|>1\): elastic
\(|E|=1\): unit
\(|E|<1\): inelastic

Revenue max at |E|=1

Break-Even Analysis

The quantity where total revenue exactly covers total cost

Every firm pays fixed costs \(FC\) regardless of output (rent, equipment, salary). Beyond that, each unit costs an additional variable cost \(vc\) per unit, so \(TC(q)=FC+vc\cdot q\). Total revenue is simply \(TR(q)=P\cdot q\).

The break-even quantity is where \(TR=TC\): \(P\cdot q=FC+vc\cdot q\Rightarrow q_{BE}=\tfrac{FC}{P-vc}\). The denominator \(P-vc\) is the contribution margin — each unit sold contributes this amount toward covering fixed costs. If the contribution margin is zero or negative, break-even is impossible.

Drag the current quantity slider to simulate your production level. The green region is profit, the orange region is loss. The margin of safety shows how far you are from break-even.

Break-Even
\(TC=FC+vc\cdot q\)
\(TR=P\cdot q\)

\(q_{BE}=\dfrac{FC}{P-vc}\)

Contribution:
\(CM=P-vc\)

Profit:
\(\pi=(P-vc)q-FC\)

Business Calculus · Chapter 5

Consumer & Producer Surplus

Welfare areas and the deadweight loss from price controls

At equilibrium price \(p^*\), every transaction is mutually beneficial: consumers pay less than their maximum willingness \(P_D(q)\) and producers receive more than their minimum acceptable \(P_S(q)\). The consumer surplus \(CS=\int_0^{q^*}[P_D(q)-p]\,dq\) and producer surplus \(PS=\int_0^{q^*}[p-P_S(q)]\,dq\) quantify these gains.

When a government sets a price floor (above equilibrium) or price ceiling (below it), the quantity traded changes and total surplus shrinks. The lost surplus is the deadweight loss (DWL) — value destroyed by the intervention. Drag the regulated price and watch DWL emerge as you move away from equilibrium.

Surplus Areas
\(CS=\int_0^{q_T}[P_D-p]\,dq\)

\(PS=\int_0^{q_T}[p-P_S]\,dq\)

\(DWL=TS^*-CS-PS\)

q_T = min(q_D, q_S)
DWL=0 at eq. price

Present Value of an Income Stream

Discounting future cash flows back to today — \(PV=\int_0^T R(t)e^{-rt}\,dt\)

Money received in the future is worth less than money today because of the opportunity cost of capital: a dollar today can be invested at rate \(r\) to become \(e^{r\cdot t}\) dollars in \(t\) years. Conversely, a dollar received at time \(t\) is worth only \(e^{-rt}\) dollars today.

The present value of a continuous income stream \(R(t)\) over horizon \(T\) is \(PV=\int_0^T R(t)e^{-rt}\,dt\). The solid dark green curve is \(R(t)\) (the actual income); the dashed lighter curve is \(R(t)e^{-rt}\) (the discounted value). The shaded area under the discounted curve is the PV. Raising \(r\) compresses the discounted curve downward — future income shrinks in present-value terms.

Present Value
\(PV=\displaystyle\int_0^T R(t)e^{-rt}\,dt\)

Constant \(R\):
\(PV=\dfrac{R}{r}(1-e^{-rT})\)

Growing \(R=R_0 e^{gt}\):
\(PV=\dfrac{R_0}{r-g}(1-e^{-(r-g)T})\)

As T→∞: PV→R/r

Profit Optimization

Maximizing profit where marginal revenue equals marginal cost

For a competitive firm the market price \(P\) is fixed, so \(MR=P\) (horizontal). The firm maximizes profit by choosing \(q^*\) where \(MC(q^*)=P\). The profit rectangle has width \(q^*\) and height \(P-ATC(q^*)\); its area is economic profit (green) or loss (orange).

For a monopolist with inverse demand \(P=a-bq\), total revenue is \(TR=aq-bq^2\) so \(MR=a-2bq\) (slopes twice as steeply as demand). The monopolist chooses \(q^*\) where \(MR=MC\), then reads the price \(p^*=a-bq^*\) off the demand curve. The gap between demand and MR curves shows the monopoly's market power.

Toggle between market structures and observe how equilibrium quantity, price, and profit differ — monopoly always produces less and charges more than the competitive outcome.

MR = MC Rule
Competitive:
\(MR=P\) (horiz.)
\(MC=TC'(q)\)

Monopoly:
\(MR=a-2bq\)
\(P=a-bq^*\)

Profit:
\(\pi=(P-ATC)q^*\)

Business Calculus · Chapter 8

Compound Growth & Continuous Compounding

As compounding frequency grows without bound, discrete growth converges to \(A=Pe^{rt}\)

If \(P\) dollars are invested at annual rate \(r\) compounded \(n\) times per year, after \(t\) years the balance is \(A_n(t)=P\!\left(1+\tfrac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}\). As \(n\to\infty\), this converges to the continuously compounded formula \(A(t)=Pe^{rt}\) — because \(\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1+\tfrac{r}{n}\right)^n=e^r\).

The graph shows all four compounding frequencies simultaneously. The differences are largest at high interest rates and long time horizons. The effective annual rate (EAR) for frequency \(n\) is \((1+r/n)^n-1\); for continuous compounding it is \(e^r-1\).

The present value formula reverses this: \(PV=Fe^{-rT}\) discounts future value \(F\) back to today. This is the foundation of Module 6.

Compounding
Discrete (n/yr):
\(A=P\!\left(1+\dfrac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}\)

Continuous:
\(A=Pe^{rt}\)

EAR:
\(e^r-1\) (continuous)

Limit: \(\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^n\to e^r\)

📈 Business Calculus

Business Calculus applies the tools of differential and integral calculus to economic decision-making: firms optimize profit, markets reach equilibrium, investors discount future cash flows, and policies create or destroy welfare. These 8 interactive modules cover the standard Business Calculus curriculum with live visualizations.

This subject is fundamentally about thinking at the margin: every business decision, from pricing to investment to hiring, is best analyzed by asking what happens to costs, revenues, and profits when you change the decision variable by one small unit.
