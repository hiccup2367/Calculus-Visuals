Calculus I · Chapter 1

Limits & Continuity

The language of approaching — not arriving

One of the foundational ideas in calculus is the limit — the value a function approaches as its input draws near to some point. Crucially, a limit describes behavior near a point, not necessarily at it. A function may fail to be defined at \(x = a\) and yet still possess a well-defined limit there.

We write \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L\) when \(f(x)\) can be made arbitrarily close to \(L\) by taking \(x\) sufficiently close to \(a\) — from both sides simultaneously. If left-hand and right-hand limits disagree, the limit does not exist (DNE).

Move \(a\) to choose an approach point and adjust \(\delta\) for the neighborhood. Try each function to experience removable holes, jump discontinuities, and vertical asymptotes.

ε-δ Definition
\(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\)

\(\forall\,\varepsilon>0,\ \exists\,\delta>0\,:\)
\(0<|x-a|<\delta\)
\(\Rightarrow|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon\)

Calculus I · Chapter 2

The Derivative

Instantaneous rate of change — the tangent line as a limit

The average rate of change of \(f\) over \([x_0,x_0+h]\) is the slope of the secant line through two points on the curve. As \(h\) shrinks toward zero, that secant pivots toward the tangent line at \(x_0\). The slope of this limiting tangent is the derivative \(f'(x_0)\).

A function is differentiable at \(x_0\) when this limit exists. Corners (like \(|x|\) at 0) are not differentiable there — left and right secant slopes approach different values, so no unique tangent can be drawn.

Click the graph to place \(x_0\), then press Animate \(h\to 0\) to watch the secant automatically converge to the tangent.

Limit Definition
\(\displaystyle f'(x_0)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x_0{+}h)-f(x_0)}{h}\)

Curve Sketching

Reading a function’s geometry through its derivatives

The first derivative \(f'(x)\) gives the slope of the curve — positive where the function rises, negative where it falls, and zero at critical points that are candidates for local maxima and minima.

The second derivative \(f''(x)\) captures concavity. Where \(f''>0\), the curve bends upward (concave up); where \(f''<0\), it bends downward. Where concavity switches, we have an inflection point.

Toggle the three curves and click the graph to move the trace point. Watch \(f'=0\) align exactly with the peaks and valleys of \(f\), and \(f''=0\) coincide with inflection points.

Key Relationships
\(f'>0\) \(\Rightarrow\) increasing
\(f'<0\) \(\Rightarrow\) decreasing
\(f'=0\) \(\Rightarrow\) critical pt

\(f''>0\) \(\Rightarrow\) concave up
\(f''<0\) \(\Rightarrow\) concave dn
\(f''=0\) \(\Rightarrow\) inflection

Optimization

Finding the largest and smallest values on a closed interval

By the Extreme Value Theorem, every continuous function on a closed interval \([a,b]\) is guaranteed to attain both a global maximum and a global minimum. This underpins applications from minimizing material cost to maximizing revenue.

The Closed Interval Method is systematic: global extrema occur only at endpoints or at critical points inside \((a,b)\) where \(f'=0\). Evaluate \(f\) at all candidates and compare.

Drag the interval bounds to redefine \([a,b]\). The same function can have different extrema on different intervals — the bounds matter critically.

Closed Interval Method
1. Find all \(c\in(a,b)\)
   where \(f'(c)=0\)

2. Evaluate \(f\) at \(a,b\)
   and each critical \(c\)

3. Largest \(=\) global max
   Smallest \(=\) global min

Calculus I · Chapter 5

Riemann Sums

Approximating area with rectangles — as \(n\to\infty\) the exact integral emerges

The area problem asks for the area bounded by \(y=f(x)\), the \(x\)-axis, and the lines \(x=a\) and \(x=b\). A Riemann sum approximates it by partitioning \([a,b]\) into \(n\) equal subintervals of width \(\Delta x=(b-a)/n\) and placing a rectangle of height \(f(x_i^*)\) on each.

Four sampling methods exist: Left, Right, Midpoint, and Trapezoid. All four converge to \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) as \(n\to\infty\), though the midpoint and trapezoid rules converge roughly 4× faster for smooth functions.

Push \(n\) to 100 and watch the error shrink. Switch between methods to compare convergence rates.

Riemann Sum
\(\displaystyle\sum_i f(x_i^*)\cdot\Delta x\)

\(\Delta x=\dfrac{b-a}{n}\)

As \(n\to\infty\,:\)
\(\displaystyle\sum\to\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\)

Error \(\to 0\) as \(n\) grows

Calculus I · Chapter 6

The Definite Integral

Net signed area — above the axis is positive, below is negative

The definite integral \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) measures signed area between the curve and the \(x\)-axis. Regions above contribute positive area; regions below contribute negative area. The integral returns the net result — they can partially or fully cancel.

If \(f(t)\) is velocity, \(\int_a^b f(t)\,dt\) gives displacement (net position change), which can be zero even if you traveled far. Total distance would require integrating \(|f(t)|\).

Adjust the bounds \(a\) and \(b\) to select the region. Dark blue marks positive area; lighter blue marks negative.

Calculus I · Chapter 7

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Differentiation and integration are inverse operations

Part 1 states that if \(F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\) is the accumulation function — the running total of signed area from \(a\) to \(x\) — then \(F'(x)=f(x)\). Differentiating the integral of \(f\) gives back \(f\) itself.

Part 2 is the computational tool: \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a)\), where \(F\) is any antiderivative. This eliminates Riemann sum limits entirely; just evaluate an antiderivative at the bounds and subtract.

Click the graph or drag \(x\) to watch \(F(x)\) accumulate signed area. \(F\) grows when \(f\) is positive, shrinks when \(f\) is negative. The slope of \(F\) at any \(x\) equals \(f(x)\).

FTC — Both Parts
Part 1:
\(\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt=f(x)\)

Part 2:
\(\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\)
\(=F(b)-F(a)\)

\(F\) = any antiderivative of \(f\)

Calculus II · Chapter 1

Taylor Series

Approximating any smooth function with polynomials centered at a point

A Taylor series approximates a smooth function near a center \(a\): \(T_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k\). The key insight is matching not just \(f(a)\), but its entire sequence of derivatives — each additional term extends the radius of accurate approximation.

Famous Maclaurin series (centered at 0) include \(\sin x = x - \frac{x^3}{6} + \frac{x^5}{120} - \cdots\) and \(e^x = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2} + \cdots\). For \(e^x\) and sin/cos the polynomial tracks the function everywhere as \(n\) grows.

Increase \(n\) to watch the solid polynomial track the dashed true function farther from center. Move \(a\) to re-center the expansion.

Taylor / Maclaurin
\(\displaystyle T_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k\)

\(\sin x = x-\tfrac{x^3}{6}+\cdots\)
\(e^x = 1+x+\tfrac{x^2}{2}+\cdots\)

Volumes of Revolution

The disk method — rotating a curve around the x-axis generates a 3D solid

When the region bounded by \(y=f(x)\) is rotated around the \(x\)-axis, it sweeps out a solid of revolution. The disk method slices this solid perpendicular to the rotation axis — each slice is a circular disk with radius \(r=f(x)\) and area \(\pi r^2\).

Integrating these disk areas gives total volume: \(V=\pi\int_a^b[f(x)]^2\,dx\). This is exact. The curve and its reflection \(-f(x)\) suggest the solid’s cross-sectional silhouette.

Click the graph or drag the slice position to move the cross-sectional disk (shown as an ellipse). Its radius equals \(f(x)\) at the slice.

Disk Method
\(\displaystyle V=\pi\int_a^b[f(x)]^2\,dx\)

Calculus II · Chapter 3

Series & Convergence

When does an infinite sum converge to a finite value?

An infinite series \(\sum a_n\) is a sum of infinitely many terms. Whether it has a finite value depends on the partial sums \(S_n=a_1+\cdots+a_n\). If \(S_n\) approaches a finite limit as \(n\to\infty\), the series converges; if not, it diverges.

Geometric series \(\sum r^n\) converge when \(|r|<1\) with exact sum \(r/(1-r)\), and diverge when \(|r|\ge1\). The \(p\)-series \(\sum 1/n^p\) converges when \(p>1\). The harmonic series (\(p=1\)) diverges — astonishingly slowly.

Watch the partial sum plot: a converging series levels off toward the green dashed limit; a diverging series climbs without bound.

Key Results
Geometric \(\sum r^n\):
\(|r|<1\Rightarrow S=\dfrac{r}{1-r}\)
\(|r|\ge1\Rightarrow\) diverges

\(p\)-series \(\sum\frac{1}{n^p}\):
\(p>1\Rightarrow\) converges
\(p\le1\Rightarrow\) diverges

📖 My Story

I have always been interested in mathematics, and as someone who has taught themselves calculus, I came to an important conclusion. While calculus may seem daunting at first, in truth, it’s the same exact thing as algebra.

The rules that we had to learn when we were first solving equations with one unknown variable are very similar to the rules of calculus you have to learn when exploring this topic: the only real difference is that algebra is a lot easier to visualize compared to calculus, which is why I chose to build this tool.

The goal here is to provide aspiring math students with the ability to visually see calculus concepts and what they measure so that they can understand these concepts more deeply.
